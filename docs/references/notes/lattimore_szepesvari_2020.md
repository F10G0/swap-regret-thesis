“A stochastic bandit is a collection of distributions ν = (Pa : a ∈ A), where A is the set of available actions. The learner and the environment interact sequentially over n rounds. In each round t ∈ {1, . . . , n}, the learner chooses an action At ∈ A, which is fed to the environment. The environment then samples a reward Xt ∈ R from distribution PAt and reveals Xt to the learner.” ([Lattimore and Szepesvári, 2020, p. 56](zotero://select/library/items/FBR6RLGD)) ([pdf](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/5URJXI9C?page=65&annotation=48RSKD9V))

“maximise the total reward Sn = ∑n t=1 Xt” ([Lattimore and Szepesvári, 2020, p. 57](zotero://select/library/items/FBR6RLGD)) ([pdf](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/5URJXI9C?page=66&annotation=AR3JMLHR))

“A policy that maximises the expectation of Sn for one bandit instance may behave quite badly on another. The learner usually has partial information about ν, which we represent by defining a set of bandits E for which ν ∈ E is guaranteed. The set E is called the environment class. We distinguish between structured and unstructured bandits.” ([Lattimore and Szepesvári, 2020, p. 58](zotero://select/library/items/FBR6RLGD)) ([pdf](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/5URJXI9C?page=67&annotation=K7JNWGAU))
